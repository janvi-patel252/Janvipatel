§ 01 · The thesis

What I actually do, in one paragraph.

Most cost errors are translation errors.

Between the drawing, the field, and the spreadsheet, something usually gets lost. A quantity nobody walked back to the plan. A line item that made sense to the estimator but not to the inspector six months later. That's where the money leaks.

My background runs both sides of that translation. I verified rebar against bar bending schedules on an active site. I audit insurance estimates under litigation deadlines. I coordinated borewells across 22 rural sites and maintenance across two university campuses. The skill is the same in every context — find where the scope and the number disagree, close the gap before someone else does.

The brief day one

Auditing insurance numbers under litigation timelines.

The firm represents homeowners whose insurance claims are in dispute. My job as an estimator: prepare reconstruction estimates that stand up in court, and audit what the insurer put on paper to find what's missing. Average claim value is $300K+. The numbers have to be defensible line by line.

Insurer estimates aren't built to be generous — they're built to be a defensible floor. That leaves gaps. Finding those gaps, pricing them correctly, and documenting them so opposing counsel can't dismiss them is the work.

The method per claim

How I work a claim, every time.

  1. 01
    Read the drawings before the estimate.
    Plan set, elevations, field photos, inspection report. Before I open Xactimate, I know the scope from the documents — not the other estimator's version of it.
  2. 02
    Quantity take-offs, trade by trade.
    Roofing, interiors, HVAC, MEP. Roof squares calculated from base SF, garage, and overhangs using pitch and waste factors. Interiors room-by-room. Nothing rounded until the final total.
  3. 03
    Audit the insurer estimate line by line.
    Every missing item flagged, every undervalued unit cost noted, every scope ambiguity documented. Omissions are where the money lives.
  4. 04
    Build AutoCAD & Revit exhibits.
    Existing-conditions drawings and repair diagrams that substantiate the scope. Visual exhibits for depositions and trial — because a line-item spreadsheet alone doesn't travel well in court.
  5. 05
    Check it twice before it ships.
    Structured review: quantities back-checked to drawings, totals verified, exclusions explicitly listed. The extra hour saves the phone call three weeks into construction.

A number without a source is a guess. In litigation, guesses get torn apart.

The brief

42,000 sq ft. Multiple blocks. All pouring in parallel.

Nishan Developers was building a 42,000+ sq ft mixed-use development. Multiple structural blocks were running in parallel — each on its own pour schedule. My job was to verify what got built matched what was drawn, and to catch deviations before concrete locked them in.

What I did

Inspection, before the pour.

  1. 01
    Checked bar bending schedules against structural drawings.
    Reinforcement spacing, lap lengths, cover blocks, anchorage zones — every pour. BBS drift is the kind of error that only shows up in a post-pour inspection, which is too late.
  2. 02
    Inspected structural geometry across parallel blocks.
    Slab thickness, beam depth, column alignment. Multiple blocks pouring concurrently means you need a system, not heroics.
  3. 03
    Caught deviations before cast.
    Fixes before the pour cost a redo of steel placement. Fixes after the pour cost a demolition, a redesign, and a schedule hit.
  4. 04
    Facilitated 18 pre-construction coordination meetings.
    Estimators, superintendents, and project managers in the same room on scope, sequencing, and scheduling.

Estimating a structural element and verifying it in the field are the same skill in opposite directions.

Results

What the role gave me.

42Ksq ft mixed-use development
$9.9MProject value
18Pre-construction meetings facilitated
0Post-pour structural reworks on my watch
What I learned

Drawings translate. Fields confirm.

Having verified structural work in the field before I ever priced it has changed how I read drawings. When I estimate a concrete element now, I know what the pour looks like — the sequencing, the access constraints, the trades that need to clear out before it goes. That shows up in the estimate.

Why this matters
Estimators who've only worked from a desk price scope. Estimators who've verified scope in the field price the work. The second is more accurate, and this role is why I can do both.

The question

Does NYC traffic volume actually drive air quality?

Everyone knows traffic hurts air quality. Fewer people know by how much, where, and when — the kind of detail that matters if a city is making an infrastructure-scale decision about congestion pricing, bus lanes, or freight routing.

I wanted to see if I could put real numbers on the relationship using public data. Not hypothesize. Measure.

What I did

End-to-end, solo.

  1. 01
    Collected data from EPA and NYC DOT (2019–2022).
    Four years of traffic counts, four years of air quality readings (PM2.5, NO₂, O₃). 650,000+ rows combined.
  2. 02
    Cleaned and merged with Pandas.
    Date standardization, location mapping, missing-value handling. Preprocessing lifted data accuracy by ~95% before any modeling happened.
  3. 03
    Built regression models to test the correlation.
    Traffic volume against each pollutant, with temporal and spatial controls. Iterated on feature selection until R² stabilized.
  4. 04
    Applied K-Means clustering to find high-impact zones.
    Three clusters emerged cleanly: Manhattan core, outer-borough arterials, residential side streets. The clusters validated visually against a map.
  5. 05
    Visualized with Matplotlib and Seaborn.
    Scatter plots, heatmaps, time-series decompositions. Built so someone without the notebook could still read the story.

Urban planning decisions get better when the relationship is measured, not assumed.

Results

What the data showed.

650K+Data points processed across 4 years
~95%Data accuracy after preprocessing
+15%Model accuracy improvement post-tuning
3Impact zones identified via K-Means

The clustering surfaced what anyone who's lived in NYC could guess — but with numbers attached. Manhattan core zones consistently paired high traffic volume with elevated PM2.5. Outer-borough arterials formed a distinct middle cluster. Residential zones sat in a separate low-impact cluster. The relationship was strongest for PM2.5 and NO₂ and weaker for O₃ — consistent with the chemistry.

What I learned

Preprocessing is the work.

The modeling took a few days. The data cleaning took weeks. Date formats inconsistent across agencies, location fields mismatched, missing readings clustered on weekends. The headline number is a regression R². The actual insight was learning how much of a data project is just getting the data to a state where it can be modeled honestly.

Why this matters
This project shows the translation skill — drawings, field, cost, data — applied to urban planning and environmental decision-making. Same muscle I use reading structural drawings, pointed at a different kind of data.

The problem

Reactive safety vs. proactive safety.

Safety on construction sites is usually reactive — an incident happens, a meeting gets called, a new rule gets written. The data to predict incidents before they occur is almost always already there. Nobody has time to model it.

I wanted to build something a safety manager could actually run. Not a PhD thesis. Excel only. No specialist software. No bespoke add-ins. If it couldn't live in a field office, it didn't count.

What I built

A safety manager's tool, not a data scientist's.

  1. 01
    Chose seven risk factors from historical incident data.
    Equipment failure, communication gaps, PPE non-compliance, weather, training gaps, fatigue, site conditions. Each factor got its own probability distribution based on historical data.
  2. 02
    Built a Monte Carlo simulation in Excel.
    10,000 runs per scenario, sampling from the seven factor distributions. Output: a distribution of likely monthly incident counts, not a single point estimate.
  3. 03
    Created 10+ dashboards tied to the simulation.
    Risk heatmaps, trend lines, factor-contribution charts. Every dashboard updated automatically when the input data changed — no re-running a model.
  4. 04
    Identified 35% of incidents linked to human error.
    Not "unpredictable" human error — predictable categories. Communication failures and training gaps were the two biggest contributors.
  5. 05
    Modeled mitigation scenarios.
    Ran the simulation with targeted mitigation (training + monitoring + improved comms) and measured the distribution shift. Predicted ~40% reduction in equipment-related failures.

The constraint was Excel. The constraint made the tool better, because the people who need to use it live in Excel.

Results

What the model predicted.

7Risk factors modeled
10+Dashboards · tied to live data
35%Of incidents linked to human error
−40%Predicted failure reduction with mitigation
What I learned

Field-ready design is harder than fancy.

The temptation on every data project is to reach for the most sophisticated tool. For this one, the discipline was the opposite — if a safety manager in a field office couldn't run it without training, it wasn't useful, no matter how elegant it was in a Jupyter notebook. The Excel constraint pushed me to simplify the model, not dumb it down.

Why this matters
This reflects my approach to safety: proactive, data-driven, and usable by the people on site. The same approach translates to estimating, scheduling, and any field-facing analytical work.

§ 04 · How I work

Five things that show up in everything I do.

Meticulous, by habit.

Not a list of rules to sound impressive — just how I actually operate, across every project on this page.

01
I double-check every number before it leaves my desk.
Back-checked against the source document — the drawing, the spec, the line item. If it can't be traced, it isn't ready.
02
Details are where the work is.
The HVAC line set. The drip edge. The baseboard in the third bedroom. Meticulous isn't a polite word for careful — it's the thing that makes the estimate actually correct.
03
I write things down as I go.
Scope notes, assumptions, exclusions, how a quantity was calculated. Not for memory — so the work is self-explanatory the next time it's picked up.
04
I read the drawings before anything else.
Plan set, elevations, field photos. Before I open Xactimate or price a line, I've built my own understanding of the scope from the source.
05
I finish what I start.
Cleanly. From first take-off to final review. A scope with unresolved items is one that costs someone later.
